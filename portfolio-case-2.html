Product Ownership for a Social Impact LLM: The Google Gemma 3B Challenge

The challenge:

The Google Gemma 3B Impact Challenge was a high-pressure, one-month innovation sprint to create a tangible, real-world application using a new LLM. The core challenge was not just technical; it was about telling a powerful story. Standard productivity apps often fail because they expect perfect consistency in a world that is anything but. Our mission was to build a tool that bends to your life instead of breaking under it.

My Role as Product Owner & Creative Designer
  • As the team's Product Owner, I was responsible for the entire project lifecycle, from initial strategy to final submission. I authored the Project Charter, defining our SMART goals, which included winning the Unsloth Special Technology Prize and achieving 2 second response times for the on-device model. My primary focus was on translating our ambitious vision into a manageable, one-month roadmap, breaking down complex AI/ML tasks into a clear, two-sprint plan.


  • Simultaneously, as the Creative Designer, I led the narrative and visual execution. I synthesized our user research into a compelling story for the final submission video and designed the complete UI/UX concept in Figma, ensuring our technical solution was presented as a simple, empathetic, and user-friendly application.

Lessons learned:
  • Conceptualizing Without Users: With a one-month deadline, traditional user research was impossible. The biggest challenge was defining a concept that was both impactful and resonant. We solved this by turning inward, using our own experiences to develop the core idea of "treating life as a project." This forced us to rapidly iterate on the concept, evolving it dramatically from a simple habit tracker to an adaptive life coach that understood buffer times, requirements, and the psychology of habit formation when real life gets in the way.

  • Ruthless Prioritization Under Pressure: With a one-month deadline, the biggest risk was scope creep. I learned to be extremely disciplined in our prioritization. This meant actively letting go of "cool" feature ideas that, while exciting, did not directly serve the core user problem or meet the competition's requirements. My focus was to say "no" to good ideas to ensure we could perfectly execute a great one that delivered maximum value.

  • Translating Complexity into a Compelling Story: Our biggest final challenge was communication. I learned that the best technology is useless if you can't explain its value. Crafting the submission video taught me how to distill a complex AI concept into a simple, human-centered narrative that resonated with a non-technical audience.
